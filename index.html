Why should we care about the air we breathe?

We interact with our environment every single day, but air is the one thing we have almost no control over. You can filter your water or choose what food to buy, but you have to breathe whatever air is right in front of you. Even though it's usually invisible, the air we take in is the most direct connection we have to our surroundings, and its makeup has a massive impact on our daily health.

As cities and industries grow, the atmosphere ends up catching the invisible byproducts of everything around us — like vehicle exhaust, manufacturing emissions, and energy production. These pollutants, especially fine particulate matter, aren't just a nuisance you breathe in and cough out. They completely bypass the body's natural defenses and get directly into the bloodstream. It turns the air from a basic human necessity into a driver for serious, long-term public health issues.

But air quality is more than just a health issue; it's a structural one. Clean air isn't distributed evenly, and bad air doesn't just happen by accident. The quality of the air we breathe is heavily shaped by historical decisions, zoning laws, and industrial priorities. Looking closely at air pollution shows us exactly how environmental burdens are handed out, which is why understanding what we are inhaling is the first step in addressing the broader systems that create these hazards in the first place.

What is PM2.5 and why is it considered a serious public health threat?

Interactive · PM2.5: The Invisible Threat Open in new tab ↗

PM2.5 refers to fine, inhalable particulate matter with a diameter of 2.5 micrometers or smaller, which typically originates from chemical reactions in the atmosphere or direct emissions from combustion sources such as power plants, motor vehicles, and wildfires [EPA]. PM2.5 is a major health concern because these particles are small enough to penetrate deep into the lungs and enter the bloodstream, where exposure is linked to asthma exacerbation, reduced lung function, heart attack, stroke, arrhythmia, and premature death, with elevated risk for children, older adults, and people with heart or lung disease [EPA], [WHO]. Because these microscopic pollutants completely bypass the body's natural respiratory defenses, long-term exposure is also associated with chronic systemic inflammation, making continuous monitoring and stringent air quality regulations essential for broad public health protection.

Thermal Inversions

Usually, the air near the ground is warmer than the air higher up in the atmosphere. Since warm air naturally rises, it carries exhaust, smoke, and other pollutants up and away from the surface where we breathe.

But during a thermal inversion, that normal cycle gets flipped. A layer of warmer air moves in high above, trapping a pocket of cooler air right down at ground level.

Think of that high, warm air layer like a giant lid over the city. When an inversion settles in, all the PM2.5 coming off of busy roads and industrial sites has nowhere to go. Instead of clearing out, the pollution just gets stuck and builds up in that trapped cool air, concentrating all those hazardous particles right where people live and breathe. [Utah DEQ]
